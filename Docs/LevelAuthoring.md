# Level Authoring Workflow

The production catalog lives at `MarbleSort/Assets/MarbleSort/Resources/Levels/levels.json`.
Runtime loading and the Unity editor use this same file, so there is one source of truth.

## Open the editor

1. In Unity, choose **Marble Sort > Level Catalog**.
2. Keep `levels.json` assigned in **Catalog JSON**.
3. Choose a level from **Selected Level**.
4. Expand **Level Authoring** and edit the conveyor, top-grid boxes, or receiver lanes.

Changes are held as an in-memory draft. Validation and solvability analysis refresh whenever an
authoring field changes. **Reload JSON** discards the draft and reloads the saved asset.

## Save and preview

- **Save Valid JSON** writes only a draft with no blocking validation errors.
- **Preview Selected Level** saves a valid unsaved draft, starts Play Mode from `Main.unity`, and
  loads the chosen level automatically.
- While Play Mode is active, **Previous**, **Load Selected Level**, and **Next** rebuild a selected
  production level from a clean gameplay state.
- **Reload Current Level** resets the current level without leaving Play Mode.

The verified selection sequence shown in **Selected Level Analysis** is a known safe order for
exposed top boxes. It is generated by deterministic search and is useful for playtesting and
reviewing a newly authored queue.

## Blocking production rules

- The catalog contains exactly five levels and the conveyor contains exactly 24 logical slots.
- Every level has a display name, stable level ID, and exactly four non-empty receiver lanes.
- Object IDs are unique within a level.
- Receiver lanes have distinct horizontal positions and positive vertical spacing.
- Top boxes occupy unique, in-bounds grid cells.
- Supported colors are `green`, `blue`, `orange`, `yellow`, and `pink`.
- For every color, receiver-box count equals top-box count multiplied by three.
- At least one exposed-box selection order must complete the level without overflowing the
  24-slot conveyor.

The same rules run during game bootstrap. An invalid catalog is therefore blocked in the editor
and rejected by the runtime rather than producing a partially playable level.

## Production difficulty curve

| Level | Colors | Visible trays | Hidden trays | Total trays | Receiver boxes | Maximum depth | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | --- |
| 1 | 2 | 4 | 2 | 6 | 18 | 2 | Teach matching while introducing a short reveal chain |
| 2 | 3 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 18 | 3 | Increase ordering pressure with fewer initial choices |
| 3 | 4 | 3 | 6 | 9 | 27 | 4 | Combine all colors with two dependent hidden rows |
| 4 | 4 | 4 | 8 | 12 | 36 | 3 | Full four-column, three-row exposure planning |
| 5 | 5 | 4 | 6 | 10 | 30 | 3 | Mix five colors, interleaved receiver queues, and a third-row Pink mystery |

Level 5 deliberately avoids adjacent receiver boxes of the same color in every lane. Its first
four receivers remain blue, green, orange, and yellow for a readable opening, while deeper colors
are interleaved to require forward planning. Pink remains gated behind its third-row mystery tray.
Any future queue edit must preserve the per-color 1:3 ratio and pass the deterministic solvability
analysis against the 24-slot conveyor.
